# Distinctive ≠ usable — a committed aesthetic can intimidate its own audience

date: 2026-06-19
scope: design / visual artifacts
rule: A bold look can scare off its own audience. Judge design for first-impression, comprehension and color-meaning, not just craft.

## What happened

First real `/design` run: divergence mode on a small-business owner's dashboard produced 4 committed
directions — Terminal Ops, Bottle-Green Bold, Swiss Ledger, Cyberpunk. All four escaped the
default attractor (the skill worked). The user then asked the skeptic to weigh **usability,
not beauty**: the product is for a non-technical small-business owner, and *"if a person gets
scared by too-complex an interface, they just leave."*

A fresh skeptic, judging all 8 renders cold against fitness-for-audience, ranked:
- ✅ Swiss Ledger (PASS) — light, labeled table columns, one red = one action, ~21:1 contrast.
- 🟡 Bottle-Green Bold (PASS-WITH-CHANGES) — premium dark, gold hero anchors the headline number.
- ❌ Terminal Ops (FAIL) — reads as an engineer's server console; NOC language; flat hierarchy.
- ❌ Cyberpunk (FAIL) — *"the most stylish and the most wrong for this user"*; cyan+magenta
  sprayed decoratively, so **color stopped encoding meaning**; worst accessibility.

The two **coolest, most-committed** directions were the two that would lose the target user
at the door. The user's instinct (pretty ≠ usable) was correct; my `/design` skeptic checklist
had not asked the question — it checked contrast/commit/coherence/fidelity but not
intimidation, 5-second comprehension, or whether color carries meaning.

## The lesson

**Distinctive is not the goal — appropriate-and-distinctive is.** A committed aesthetic that
intimidates or confuses its own audience is the *wrong* kind of distinctive ("beautiful but
loses users at the door"). The escape-the-default machinery can over-shoot into
escape-the-user. Two design-failure modes the craft checklist misses:
- **Intimidation / comprehension** — does the stated audience feel "this is for me" in the
  first glance, and understand what matters / what to do in 5 seconds?
- **Color encodes meaning, not just style** — a signal color must mean ONE thing (red = the
  one action); loud accents sprayed decoratively means nothing reads as "this is what matters."

A "cool" comp can be a `FAIL` even at high craft. Name the audience up front and judge against
them — not against an abstract "is it striking?"

## How to apply

- `/design` Stage 2 (gate) now requires each proposed direction to **fit the audience**, not
  just be distinctive; name the audience in Stage 1.
- `/design` Stage 5 (skeptic) now includes first-impression/intimidation, 5-sec comprehension,
  and color-encodes-meaning checks, and "distinctive-but-intimidating for its audience" is an
  explicit FAIL condition. (Folded in same-day.)
- A great direction the skeptic rejects for *this* product is not waste — keep it in the
  catalog (`styles/`) for the right surface/audience. Cyberpunk stays proven for landing
  pages / power-users; it just isn't a non-technical owner's daily dashboard.
